No Radio Afterglow from the Gamma-Ray Burst of 1997 February 28

D. A. FRAIL, 1 S. R. KULKARNI, 2 D. S. SHEPHERD, 2 AND E. WAXMAN 3

Received 1998 April 16; accepted 1998 June 2; published 1998 July 9


ABSTRACT

     We present radio observations of the gamma-ray burster GRB 970228 made with the Very Large Array and the Owens Valley Radio Observatory which span a range of postburst timescales from 1 to 300 days. A search for a time-variable radio source was conducted covering an area that included a fading X-ray source and an optical transient, both of which are thought to be the long-wavelength counterparts to the gamma-ray burst. At the position of the optical transient, sensitive limits between 10 Jy and 1 mJy can be placed on the absence of a radio counterpart to GRB 970228 between 1.4 and 240 GHz. We apply a simple formulation of a fireball model which has been used with some success to reproduce the behavior of the optical and X-ray light curves. Using this model, we conclude that the radio nondetections are consistent with the peak flux density of the afterglow lying between 20 and 40 Jy, and it requires that the optical flux peaked between 4 and 16 hr after the burst.

§1. INTRODUCTION

     The gamma-ray burst of 1997 February 28 marked a turning point in our understanding of these enigmatic objects, resulting in the first-ever discovery of X-ray and optical counterparts to a burst. Within the original 3 localization provided by the Wide Field Cameras (WFCs) on board the BeppoSAX satellite, a previously unknown X-ray source 1SAX J0501.7+1146 was detected by the Narrow Field Instruments (NFI). Eight hours after the burst, the flux of 1SAX J0501.7+1146 was 2.8×10 ergs cm-2 s-1 (210 keV), but 3 days later its flux had dropped by a factor of 20 (Costa et al. 1997). A comparison of V- and I-band images taken on 1997 February 28 and March 8 revealed an optical transient within a reduced error box defined by the intersection of the WFC circle, the ±50 circle of the NFI, and the Interplanetary Network (IPN) annulus (van Paradijs et al. 1997; Hurley et al. 1997).

     Predictions of long-lived afterglows from gamma-ray bursts at X-ray, optical, and radio wavelengths have been made for some time (e.g., Paczyski & Rhoads 1993; Mészáros, Rees, & Papathanassiou 1994; Katz 1994; Mészáros & Rees 1997). In particular, a power-law decay in the observed long-wavelength flux with time is a generic consequence of a class of models known as "fireballs." The gamma rays are thought to be produced when the relativistically expanding blast wave (i.e., fireball) is slowed down by the ambient gas or is self-shocked by its own ejecta. The fireball accelerates particles in a shock, which then radiate via the synchrotron process (Mészáros et al. 1994; Waxman 1997a, 1997b; Sari, Piran, & Narayan 1998). As the fireball expands it cools, shifting the peak in the spectrum to lower energies and resulting in delayed emission at longer wavelengths.

     The optical and X-ray decay from GRB 970228 as well as that from several other subsequent gamma-ray bursts is consistent at least to first order with one of the simpler formulations of these models (Mészáros & Rees 1997). Costa et al. (1997) fit a t decay to the X-ray data with -1.33±0.12, while global fits to the X-ray, optical, and infrared data give =-1.2 (Wijers, Rees, & Mészáros 1997) and =-1.09±0.23 (Reichart 1997). More recent optical fits, aided by a long time baseline, yield =-1.22±0.03 (Garcia et al. 1997), =-1.21±0.02 (Masetti et al. 1998), and =-1.10±0.04 (Galama et al. 1998). The character of this decay agrees well with a fireball produced by a one-time impulsive injection of energy in which only the forward blast wave efficiently accelerates particles (i.e., the adiabatic piston model). The adiabatic model predicts a simple relation between the slope of the temporal decay and the flux spectrum =2/3. Observationally the spectrum is not well determined, with =-0.7±0.1 optically (van Paradijs et al. 1997) and X-ray values of -0.9 with large scatter (Frontera et al. 1998). The slope of the particle spectrum p [where =(p+1)/2] inferred from these values of temporal decay is p-2.6, which is not an unreasonable value for a relativistic shock (Blandford & Eichler 1987).

     Given the early success of the fireball model in predicting the gross properties of the optical and X-ray behavior of GRB 970228, it is reasonable to look for delayed radio emission from this burst. This is particularly relevant in light of the discovery of the radio afterglow from GRB 970508 (Frail et al. 1997a), which exhibited temporal and spectral behavior consistent with this model (Waxman, Kulkarni, & Frail 1997). The properties of the fireball for GRB 970228 are well constrained by the optical and X-ray data. Thus, the presence or absence of radio emission at late times has a bearing on the validity of this model. With this in mind, we began a radio search centered on the optical transient detected by van Paradijs et al. (1997). This Letter is a summary of our monitoring program for the first 300 days.

§2. OBSERVATIONS AND RESULTS

     Radio observations of a field centered on GRB 970228 began with the Very Large Array (VLA) on 1997 March 1.03 UT, 22 hr after the burst. Initially the VLA observations were made at a frequency of 1.43 GHz (=21 cm), at which the field of view is large enough to accommodate the preliminary 10 radius error circles from BeppoSAX. As the position of GRB 970228 became better known, we increased the observing frequency first to 4.63 GHz (=6.5 cm) and then to 8.46 GHz (=3.5 cm), beginning 3 days after the first VLA observation. On 1997 March 16.09 UT, a full synthesis single track was made with the millimeter interferometer at the Owens Valley Radio Observatory (OVRO) at 92.7 GHz (=3.2 mm) and 242.2 GHz (=1.2 mm). See Shepherd et al. (1998) for more details on the OVRO observation.

     The results of our VLA and OVRO observing campaign of GRB 970228, which spanned postburst timescales from 1 to 300 days, are summarized in Table 1 and are plotted in Figure 1. The dates of each observation are given followed by the number of elapsed days, the observing frequency, the synthesized beam size, and the rms noise on each image. The last column in Table 1 quantifies our principal scientific result, which is that between 1 and 300 days after the initial gamma-ray event no radio emission was detected from GRB 970228. One radio source was detected in the ±50 radius of the NFI (Frail et al. 1997b), but it lies outside the reduced IPN/WFC/NFI error box and has spectral and temporal characteristics that are consistent with its being an unrelated background extragalactic object. At the location of the van Paradijs et al. (1997) optical transient, sensitive 1 limits between 10 Jy and1 mJy can be placed on the absence of a radio counterpart to GRB 970228 between 1 and 250 GHz. The limits at 8.46 GHz are the most severe, with typical 2 limits of 100 Jy. Adding the 8.46 GHz data from all 14 epochs together produces a2 upper limit of 9 Jy. The Westerbork Radio Synthesis Telescope (WSRT) and the Berkeley-Illinois-Maryland Array (BIMA) also observed GRB 970228 at 5 and 86.4 GHz, respectively, producing upper limits a factor of 2 or more above those in Table 1 (Groot et al. 1997; Smith et al. 1997).

§3. DISCUSSION

§3.1. The Adiabatic Piston Model

     In this section, we will briefly review the spectral and temporal behavior for the impulsive, forward shockdominated fireball model. It is treated in far more detail by Mészáros & Rees (1997) and Waxman (1997a, 1997b). We will not use radiative models (Vietri 1997b), since they have been shown to be inconsistent with the radio observations (Waxman et al. 1997). The simplest version of the adiabatic model is used, and we will not treat the case of rapid electron cooling (Sari et al. 1998). Since the optical light curve of GRB 970228 has maintained its power-law slope with no break even 6 months after the initial detection (e.g., Garcia et al. 1997), it suggests that the slow cooling adiabatic model is an appropriate description of this system. Moreover, the absence of such a break suggests that the fireball is not far from being spherically symmetric (Rhoads 1998) (i.e., that it is not a jet).

     Initially the fireball ejecta have a bulk Lorentz factor =1010 which falls as t, where t is elapsed time in the observer reference frame. The blast wave ahead of the ejecta accelerates a power-law distribution of electrons with a minimum Lorentz factor and a power-law index of p. The emergent spectrum in the comoving frame has a synchrotron break at a frequency B (where B is the comoving magnetic field). Most of the radiation is emitted near the synchrotron break frequency. Above , the spectral slope =(1+p)/2 is set by the shock processes. Below , the spectral slope is predicted to be 1/3 from synchrotron theory (Pacholczyk 1970; Katz 1994), but it could be flatter if there are a range of physical conditions (i.e., B, ) in the shock.

     The above behavior assumes that the emission emerges from an optically thin fireball. Synchrotron self-absorption was predicted (Katz 1994; Katz & Piran 1997) to be important at low radio frequencies during the first few weeks of the fireball's existence. For GRB 970508, Shepherd et al. (1998) observed a low-frequency turnover radio spectrum of the afterglow during the first week, consistent with synchrotron self-absorption with an optical depth of 1 near 1.6 GHz. Frail et al. (1997a) observed a gradual rise in the 1.43 GHz flux at later times that is consistent with the fireball becoming optically thin at this frequency. Early estimates erroneously used a synchrotron self-absorption frequency of 1013 Hz (e.g., Mészáros & Rees 1997), but later corrections have shifted to between 1 and 5 GHz (Waxman 1997b). In this Letter it is pointed out that the addition a low-energy population of electrons below can increase the synchrotron opacity without increasing F, provided that these electrons are small in number compared to the high-energy population. While this increases somewhat, the fact remains that beyond 12 weeks after the burst the fireball emission should be optically thin at 8.46 GHz, where most of our observations have been made.

§3.2. The Radio Data and Constraints on the Fireball Model

     To first order, the model described in § 3.1 gives a fairly good description of the behavior of the X-ray and optical afterglow from GRB 970228. We will now use it to predict the late-time behavior of the afterglow at radio wavelengths. A simple-minded extrapolation of the spectral slope , measured at the time at which the optical and X-ray transients were detected, would predict flux densities in the centimeter range of 10100 mJy. Such flux densities were clearly not seen, so we can infer that at this time the radio band was below . Frequencies below 5 GHz may have been initially strongly self-absorbed (i.e., <<), but this would not be the case for the majority of our data at 8.46 GHz nor our OVRO data at millimeter wavelengths (Waxman 1997b).

     The radio emission peaks at F at a time t, delayed from the optical peak given by t=t(/). For =8.46 GHz, t1400t, and for =92.7 GHz, t300t. Neither F nor t are well constrained by the X-ray and optical afterglows since a distinct turnover was not observed. One approach used by Wijers et al. (1997) is to use the properties of the gamma-ray burst to constrain the values of F and t for the fireball. There is no a priori reason to assume that the gamma-ray burst and the fireball are directly related in this way. In fact, it is currently held that the two are physically decoupled from one other (e.g., Mészáros 1998).

     Nevertheless, if we adopt t670 s for R band used by Wijers et al., we find that the predicted radio emission from GRB 970228 would peak at 106 s, thereafter decaying in the same manner as the optical and X-ray. At 8.46 GHz, we have sensitive upper limits for the absence of radio emission at the location of the optical transient, spanning approximately 2 orders of magnitude in time (105107 s). For the value of F5000 Jy and the t-1.2 decay fit for > from Wijers et al. (1997), the VLA would have been expected to detect a bright radio source during its entire monitoring interval (greater than 100 Jy). Our millimeter observations at OVRO are less constraining. At the time of these observations, nearly 2 weeks after the burst, the millimeter emission from GRB 970228 is expected to have decayed below detectablity, and thus it cannot provide any useful constraints on the fireball model. Wijers et al. (1997) assume =0 for GRB 970228, so the flux is constant and equal to F until =. A more conservative approach is to set to the asymptotic value of 1/3, so that the flux rises as t1/2. This is still at odds with our radio data, and we conclude that the estimates of F and t used by Wijers et al. are incorrect.

     A compilation of flux measurements for the optical transient are given by Galama et al. (1997) and updated in Galama et al. (1998). The earliest measurement was by Pedichini et al. (1997) on February 28.81 UT, which was translated to an R-band magnitude of 20.5±0.5 (Galama et al. 1997). A similar but more accurate value of 20.9±0.14 was measured on February 28.99 UT by van Paradijs et al. (1997). Converting these R-band magnitudes to flux densities, we derive values of 18 and 12±2 Jy, respectively. No clear signature of a turnover has been seen in the optical data, and in fact the power-law decay of GRB 970228 has maintained its character with small deviations beginning 16 hr after the burst to the present time (Galama et al. 1998). Thus the peak flux density must have been prior to this time, for which we derive F18 Jy and t6×10 s.

The absence of readily detectable radio emission 300 days after the original event requires t>2.8×10 s and F<40 Jy. Similarly, an upper limit on t is set by the date of the earliest detection of optical emission from GRB 970228 of 18 Jy at 6×10 s by Pedichini et al. (1997). If the adiabatic fireball model is to remain a valid description of the afterglow emission from GRB 970228, then it predicts that the radio afterglow will peak around 1840 Jy between 2×10 and 8×10 s. Castander & Lamb (1998) have argued in favor of visual extinction A=1.3 toward GRB 970228, a value some 23 times larger than used in most papers to date. This has the effect of raising F, making the radio nondetections even more problematic. The radio emission may be weaker than these predictions when the decelerating fireball eventually becomes subrelativistic (Wijers et al. 1997). However, such a transition should first be seen in the optical light curve as a break in the power-law slope, and no such break has been seen thus far.

§4. CONCLUSIONS

     The detection of an optical transient, thought to be the afterglow from GRB 970228, has made it possible to perform a search for time-variable radio emission at the optical position. Delayed emission at longer wavelengths (X-ray, optical, and radio) is a generic prediction of all fireball models. VLA and OVRO observations have been made that span a range of postburst timescales from 1 to 300 days, putting limits on the absence of a radio counterpart to GRB 970228 between 10 Jy and 1 mJy. Applying a simple version of the fireball model that has been used successfully to fit the temporal behavior of the decaying X-ray and optical emission from GRB 970228 suggests that the radio afterglow has yet to peak. If this is correct, then continued deep imaging in the coming months offers the possibility for the detection of a weak but increasing radio signal. Detecting this emission would be a powerful verification of the fireball model as described by Mészáros & Rees (1997).

     Indeed, as the recent detection of GRB 970508 has taught us, radio afterglows yield unique gamma-ray burst diagnostics that are not obtainable by any other means (Frail et al. 1997b). Unlike optical or X-ray wavelengths, one is presented with the possibility of following the full evolution of the fireball emission through all of its different stages; first, while it is optically thick, then as it slowly rises to a peak flux density and thereafter decays, making a transition from an ultrarelativistic to subrelativistic shock. Furthermore, both the scintillation of the radio source (Goodman 1997) and its flux density, when it is synchrotron self-absorbed (Katz 1994), allow a determination of the size and expansion of the fireball. If the radio afterglow is going to be detected from GRB 970228 and others like it, then continued long-term monitoring is going to be required at the microjansky level.
